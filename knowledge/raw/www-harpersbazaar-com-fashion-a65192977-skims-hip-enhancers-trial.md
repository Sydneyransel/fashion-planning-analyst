First, they launched the nipple bras. Then, they released their compression shorts with extra padding at the butt. Now, Skims has iterated yet again on its body-altering accoutrements. Earlier this month, the Kardashian-founded underwear, loungewear, and shapewear brand released [“hip-enhancing” shorts](https://skims.wsktbf.net/c/3006986/618913/10056?u=https%3A%2F%2Fskims.com%2Fproducts%2Fskims-body-hip-enhancing-padded-short-onyx&subId1=har&subId3=xid:{xid}) that swap butt padding for hip padding, so you can “get the hips you’ve always wanted.”

The press release continues to describe the garment: “Designed with soft foam pads embedded at the hip, this mid-level compression solution creates a fuller hip shape and hourglass figure while accentuating your curves.” Most sizes have sold out—a few skin tones are completely gone—with smaller sizes going first.

“It’s just the sides? Like those panniers?” asked writer Heather Radke, author of _Butts: A Backstory_, when I spoke to her about this new Skims special. She’s referring to a form of undergarment with side hoops, fashionable in the courtly society of the 17th century, that greatly extends the width of skirts, but only at the sides. To have wide hips without a correspondingly larger butt is, naturally, a very rare occurrence.

![fashion display featuring a woman in elegant attire on a staircase](https://hips.hearstapps.com/hmg-prod/images/screenshot-2025-06-25-at-3-08-09-pm-685c49243948e.png?resize=980:*)

getty images

“It's a very forties silhouette,” she says, referring to the “New Look,” a silhouette popularized by Christian Dior after the Second World War. The shape emphasizes feminine curves—a full bust and hips, and a small waist—often achieved through jackets with padded hips and full skirts. It declared a post-war return to femininity, a notion that has since been both celebrated and dubbed regressive.

Stereotypically, “wide hips are so associated with fertility,” says Radke, though science has disproved the idea that wide hips aid in the act of childbirth. Drag queens are also often known to perform with augmented hips. For her book, Radke interviewed two men, a drag queen and his partner, who make hip and butt pads for fellow queens. “They're creating a female silhouette to put onto a male body,” she said. “And in that way it pushes us to examine what femininity is.”

This same exploration of femininity was on view in a number of Fall 2025 runway collections, from Colleen Allen to Bally to Alaïa. The shift from padded shoulders to [padded hips](https://www.harpersbazaar.com/fashion/fashion-week/a64050009/power-shoulders-power-hips-fall-2025-runway/) was a move from corporate tropes to those of femininity, albeit with much less traditionally feminine shapes. Bally took a more futuristic pointed shape. while Alaïa embraced surrealist tube-like add-ons. The irony is that these moments of commentary on feminine ideals appeared on ultra-thin models, further emphasizing fashion’s ongoing body diversity crisis.

![a model showcasing a bold fashion design with a unique oversized collar and fringe detailing](https://hips.hearstapps.com/hmg-prod/images/alaia-sf25-look-front-09-685c49c4ada4e.jpeg?resize=980:*)

Courtesy of Alaia

After exhausting all means of analysis on the subject, I decided to try these shorts for myself. Pulling them on—on a very hot day, might I add—I was reminded of the compression element. Lest you forget, Skims is squeezing your waist in addition to padding your hips; in their world, big hips only exist relative to a small waist.

Walking around, I did feel extra conscious of the space I was inhabiting. But I think I might be tapping into the cognitive dissonance of body distortion; I’m used to my own figure and its diameter. Sizing myself up in the mirror with my hip-enhancers tucked beneath a jersey slip dress—stretchy fabrics are key for fit here—I felt I looked…exaggerated. I’d say I have full-ish hips for my general proportions. By adding to them and nothing else, I’d created a silhouette that I probably couldn’t achieve without artificial augmentation.

![a person poses in a black dress with a pearl necklace showcasing different angles](https://hips.hearstapps.com/hmg-prod/images/camille-dip1-685c789163d47.jpg?resize=980:*)

courtesy of camille freestone

Before (left) and after (right) adding Skims padded hips

There is something to reckon with whenever you enhance your own figure: It immediately makes you more conscious of your own womanhood. It's especially interesting in relation to the body that fashion—like capital-F runway fashion—has deemed cool in the past few decades. The industry fetishizes thinness, specifically a child-like boyish figure with few to no curves. Of the 8,763 looks presented across the four major cities of Spring 2025 fashion weeks, only 0.8 per cent were plus-size (US size 14+), 4.3 per cent were mid-size (US size 6-12) and 94.9 per cent were straight-size (US size 0-4), according to a [report conducted by _Vogue Business_](https://www.voguebusiness.com/story/fashion/the-vogue-business-spring-summer-2025-size-inclusivity-report). It’s a bias that has absolutely polluted my own mind, much as I’d like to think it hasn’t. When it comes to my own style, I do often drape over the more feminine, or curvaceous, aspects of my body. The Skims forced me to do just the opposite.

Even aside from the more problematic aspects of my subconscious, I was reminded how much my outfits are in constant communication with my body. I do not champion any sort of prescriptive dress-for-your-body-type messaging. However, I’ve learned what I like on my body, what makes me feel good. That, in turn, shapes the silhouettes I gravitate toward and, on a larger scale, my sense of style.

Whether it comes from my relationship with my shape or not, I often like a really boyish silhouette. Then, I can dole out femininity purposefully in things like ladylike handbags, an unexpected sequin, or kitten heels. A padded hip, set against the fit of all my existing clothes, stretched my garments and shifted their normal place on my femininity spectrum, throwing off my preordained outfit formulas.

![comparison of two outfits featuring a white tshirt and blue jeans](https://hips.hearstapps.com/hmg-prod/images/camille-dip2-685c78552aa91.jpg?resize=980:*)

courtesy of camille freestone

Before (left) and after (right) adding Skims padded hips

I’m a fanatic about style—it's inherent in my line of work—so I’m focused on the minutiae in this argument. The Skims padded hips likely wouldn't be that evident to someone who doesn’t know me that well, or who doesn’t obsess over the lines and proportions of clothing like I do. Still, it’s interesting to confront how much fit, form, and gender expression play into personal style.

Are there reasons to wear the Skims padded hips that I did not uncover in my brief encounter? Of course, particularly when you consider that I'm someone who is comfortable with my general gender presentation. Will I wear them outside this trial? I can pretty confidently say no, though I don’t regret the exercise. Are we perhaps giving Skims a bit too much credit in this conversation about bodies, beauty standards, and gender identity? Almost definitely—but the Kardashians have a way of doing this to us, elevating seemingly banal, even potentially offensive, discourse to philosophical debate.

“For the last 15 or so years, the Kardashians, Kim in particular, have been pushing our ideas of what a body is, what we do to a body,” reflects Radke. “She is constantly provoking us, often in ways that make us uncomfortable, that are racialized, that are hyper-feminine. But she does ask us over and over again to consider what is beautiful, what is allowed, what is feminine.”
