Kim Kardashian West is just like you and me. Really. “I honestly didn't know what to expect when Covid-19 took over our world,” she says, speaking by phone from Los Angeles, summing up the thoughts of most of us during 2020. “In general — and in business.”

In business terms, it’s worked out pretty well. In June, Kardashian West sold a 20 per cent stake in her beauty brand KKW Beauty to beauty conglomerate Coty for a tidy $200 million. And her shapewear brand, Skims, has sold some three million units since its launch in September 2019. Even an initial misstep — the original ill-considered brand name of Kimono had to be rapidly withdrawn — proved no more than a glitch.

The first hint of a move into the shapewear market dates back to January 2019 when Kardashian West flew to Miami for a Met Gala fitting with designer Manfred Thierry Mugler. An Instagram post of herself, with Mugler, showed her wearing nothing but a nude tank top and cycling shorts. What the 1.8 million people who liked the post didn’t know was that Kardashian West was wearing early samples of Skims.

The brand has signalled a buzzy new era for shapewear. It features nine shades and sizing from XXS to XXXXXL, all wrapped around the alluring marketing clout of one of the world’s top media celebrities (count those 190 million Instagram followers) — attracting a broader audience than a typical shapewear brand. Skims has built an Instagram following of 2.1 million in double-quick time.

Kardashian West is keen to focus on the design qualities of the collection as much as her marketing appeal. “In the past, people might assume that shapewear is more like girdles — something that’s extremely uncomfortable,” she says. “I’ve worn corsets for events, I know how uncomfortable it is. That’s not the vibe of what I wanted.”

Kardashian West highlights the brand’s design emphasis on comfort, functionality and accessible casual style. Skims quickly evolved beyond the initial focus on core shapewear, helping to propel sales through the pandemic.

“We started Skims with the intention of being a shapewear brand,” she says, “but now it’s so much more — it has definitely exceeded our expectations.” A total of 32 collections were launched in its first year, spanning lingerie, loungewear and accessories such as body tape.

Kardashian West has also built on her exceptional connection with a younger generation of consumers. “The Kardashians have a Midas touch, particularly with millennials and Gen Z,” says Euromonitor’s fashion and beauty analyst Nina Marston. “This, along with the brand’s inclusive marketing and the casualisation of dress codes, contributes heavily to Skims’s success.”

## **Growing during Covid-19**

Demand for stay-at-home casual style has soared during the pandemic. Kardashian West’s aim was to create a second generation of shapewear, focused on comfort, says Jens Grede, Skims partner and board director. “Skims turned a category previously only bought for singular, special occasions into an everyday solution to wear under regular clothes.”

Shapewear — or “solutionwear” as Skims markets it — is central to the company’s assortment and marketing, in particular the fast-selling bodysuits. But the expansion into other product categories has also been impressive.

Kardashian West acknowledges a touch of good fortune in the timing of the move into loungewear. “I feel fortunate that we had launched our first loungewear set just a couple of months before the pandemic hit. I think that’s what people really want to wear,” she says.

The expansion across product categories allows consumers to build a capsule of neutral basics that they can mix and match. “That’s why we do a lot of the same skin tones,” says Kardashian West. “If you like a bra from one collection and you want matching underwear or shapewear, that’ll be available for you.”

Nina Marston of Euromonitor says Skims is changing the shapewear market. “It’s ’leisurie’, lingerie or shapewear that you can wear under clothes or on their own, inside and out of the house.” The global underwear market, which includes shapewear, is still expected to decline by 11 per cent in 2020 to $107 billion, according to Euromonitor, but that is a smaller decline than general apparel.

For shapewear brands, business remains challenging. Shapewear new arrivals at US online retailers are down 15 per cent this year compared with 2019, according to retail market intelligence company Edited. Legacy shapewear players in particular are seeing sales decline, driving prices down across the category to boost sell-through, according to Kayla Marci, market analyst at Edited. US retailers stocking shapewear brands Spanx, Wacoal, Miraclesuit and Maidenform are seeing increased discounts (+13 per cent) and reduced sell-through (-10 per cent) in the category.

## **Drop model drives consumer sales**

Shapewear has tended to change little from year to year in design terms. That’s where Skims’s use of the drop model has changed the parameters — drawing on the similar model of KKW Beauty. Skims products sell out more rapidly than other players, says Marci. Discounting has proven unnecessary.

Whether or not it is a successful marketing gambit, Kardashian West insists the ultra-rapid sell-out of product has taken her by surprise. “It’s just honestly a demand issue, which is the best problem to have, obviously,” she says. “Every time we think that we order a big enough order, once people start receiving the products and feel the quality, by the time we restock, it sometimes sells out even quicker than the initial launch.” Skims has had over three million waitlist sign-ups since launch, according to the brand.

## **The sweet spot of price**

Pricing has also been well judged. Kardashian West says she pitched Skims at the most affordable price point possible without compromising on fabrics. “Skims is aspirational,” adds Marci. “This price point is the sweet spot, especially for the younger generation for whom influencer culture and product is so important.”

The construction, fabric, look and feel of Skims appeal to luxury shoppers, says Jeannie Lee, head of womenswear at UK department store group Selfridges. “Without question it has exposed us to a much wider audience via the reach and influence of the brand’s social media — the response has been nothing short of complete mania.” Skims “Cosy” casualwear sold out within an hour. A number of shapewear styles, including bodysuits and cycling shorts, are sold out on the Selfridges website.

The expansion into the UK is the first in a number of international launches, says Kardashian West. “We are looking to expand to stores everywhere, the Middle East, Asia. We have a whole rollout plan,” she says.

## **Emphasising body inclusivity**

The body-positivity movement has pushed [diversity and inclusion](https://www.mckinsey.com/featured-insights/diversity-and-inclusion/diversity-wins-how-inclusion-matters#) front-of-mind for consumers, prompting a debate about shapewear and the need for restrictive clothing.

“Before, shapewear was assumed to be more of an older garment, something that was more taboo, that you wouldn’t really talk about,” she says. “Skims is about normalising shapewear and showing the younger generation how it can be worn so many ways.”

In September, Skims was criticised on social media for launching maternity shapewear. “There were some people that didn’t understand what it was really trying to do,” says Kardashian West. “I wanted it to be extra comfortable and secure to provide support.”

Not so long ago, shapewear was not considered particularly body positive or feminist, says Euromonitor’s Marston. “Skims and other emerging players have really managed to turn the message around and promote this idea of body inclusivity.”

Young consumers also [care about how brands react](https://www.voguebusiness.com/consumers/marketing-to-gen-z-during-covid-19) to the pandemic. Recognising its status as a rare success story through Covid-19, Skims donated $1 million across a range of charities including the LA Regional Food Bank and the National Domestic Workers Alliance. “Since we’re doing so well, it was really important to make a donation and give back to our community,” says Kardashian West.

For its one-year anniversary campaign, Kardashian West enlisted Gen Z favourites like TikToker Addison Rae alongside fans of Skims to pose in individual billboard campaigns showing the product on people of all ages, sizes and ethnicities.

What’s next? Kardashian West hopes to continue broadening the reach of her “solutionwear”, while launching more casualwear categories. “I just want to show more people that shapewear isn’t your grandma’s secret. It’s something that people of any generation can really wear.”
